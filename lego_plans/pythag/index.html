Pythagorean Triples

By the Pythagorean Theorem, all right triangles have the property that the sum of the squares of the two base legs' lengths is equal to the square of the length of the hypotenuse. This is most often expressed as \(a^2 + b^2 = c^2\), where \(a\) and \(b\) are the lengths of the two base legs and \(c\) is the length of the hypotenuse.

Positive whole number values of \(a\), \(b\), and \(c\) such that \(a^2 + b^2 = c^2\) are known as pythagorean triples. Each pythagorean triple \((a, b, c)\) corresponds to a right triangle with side lengths \(a\), \(b\), and \(c\).

For any pythagorean triple \((a, b, c)\), \((ka, kb, kc)\) is also a pythagorean triple for any positive whole number k. Pythagorean triples that aren't just multiples of smaller pythagorean triples (in other words, pythagorean triples where \(a\), \(b\), and \(c\) do not share a common factor*) are known as primitive pythagorean triples. For example, \((3,4,5)\) is a primitive pythgorean triple, since 3, 4, and 5 do not share a common factor*. On the other hand, \((6,8,10)\) is not a primitive pythagorean triple since 6, 8, and 10 all share a common factor of 2.

*Other than 1, of course.
